Threshold Rules for the Discounted I.I.D. Prophet Inequality

Jiechen Zhang

June 2026

Problem setting

Let \(0\le\gamma\le1\), and let \(X_1,X_2,\ldots\) be i.i.d. nonnegative random variables with common distribution \(F\). The observed discounted reward at time \(i\) is

\[ Z_i:=\gamma^{i-1}X_i. \]

We use the convention \(\gamma^0=1\). When \(\gamma=0\), only the first observation matters. The main discounted argument treats \(0\le\gamma<1\); the endpoint \(\gamma=1\) is the undiscounted i.i.d. problem and is discussed separately near the end.

The prophet receives

\[ \M:=\sup_{i\ge1}Z_i = \sup_{i\ge1}\gamma^{i-1}X_i. \]

We assume \(\E[\M]<\infty\). If \(\E[\M]=0\), every guarantee below is trivial. Since \(\M\ge X_1\), we also have \(\E[X_1]<\infty\). When \(0<\gamma<1\), Borel-Cantelli gives \(Z_i\to0\) almost surely (indeed, for every \(\varepsilon>0\), \(\sum_i\Prob(\gamma^{i-1}X_i>\varepsilon)<\infty\) by \(\E[X_1]<\infty\)), so the supremum defining \(\M\) is attained almost surely; when \(\gamma=0\), \(\M=X_1\).

Let \(\tau\ge0\) be a deterministic threshold. The algorithm stops at the first index \(i\) such that \(Z_i>\tau\). Define

\[ I_i:=\mathbf{1}\{Z_i>\tau\}\prod_{j<i}\mathbf{1}\{Z_j\le\tau\}, \qquad \ALG_\tau:=\sum_{i\ge1}Z_iI_i. \]

Since \(I_i=1\) implies \(Z_i>\tau\), we have \(Z_iI_i=\tau I_i+(Z_i-\tau)^+I_i\). Summing over \(i\) and using \(\sum_i I_i=\mathbf{1}\{\M>\tau\}\) yields the threshold/surplus decomposition below.

For \(0\le\gamma<1\), the goal is to choose a deterministic fixed threshold \(\tau\) satisfying

\[ \E[\ALG_\tau]\ge \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \]

For a threshold \(\tau\), define the prophet tail and prophet surplus by

\[ p(\tau):=\Prob(\M>\tau), \qquad S(\tau):=\E[(\M-\tau)^+]. \]

Throughout, we first assume that ties occur with probability zero; the general case follows by independent infinitesimal tie-breaking perturbations.

Threshold decomposition

We use the same threshold/surplus framework as in the Threshold Rules for the Classical Prophet Inequality: split the realized payoff into the threshold part and the surplus above the threshold, then certify the surplus. The discounted setting uses this framework with \(Z_i\) in place of \(X_i\), and the geometric discount supplies the extra surplus estimate. For the proof below, assume \(0\le\gamma<1\). Taking expectations in the threshold/surplus decomposition gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \E[\ALG_\tau] &= \underbrace{\E[\tau\mathbf{1}\{\M>\tau\}]}_{\text{threshold part}} + \underbrace{\E\left[\sum_{i\ge1}(Z_i-\tau)^+I_i\right]}_{\text{surplus part}} \\ &= p(\tau)\tau+ \E\left[\sum_{i\ge1}(Z_i-\tau)^+I_i\right]. \end{aligned} \tag{1}\label{eq:discounted-threshold-surplus} \]

Thus it remains to certify the surplus term. Write

\[ c_i(\tau):=\E[(Z_i-\tau)^+]. \]

Since the event of reaching time \(i\) is independent of \(Z_i\), the threshold surplus term is

\[ \E\left[\sum_{i\ge1}(Z_i-\tau)^+I_i\right] = \sum_{i\ge1} \Prob(Z_1\le\tau,\ldots,Z_{i-1}\le\tau)c_i(\tau). \tag{2}\label{eq:exact-discounted-surplus} \]

Set

\[ C(\tau):=\sum_{i\ge1}c_i(\tau). \]

Since the maximum surplus is at most the sum of the coordinate surpluses,

\[ S(\tau)=\E[(\M-\tau)^+] \le \sum_{i\ge1}\E[(Z_i-\tau)^+] = C(\tau). \tag{3}\label{eq:surplus-sum-dominates} \]

Also, for every \(i\), reaching time \(i\) is at least as likely as the event that no discounted value ever crosses the threshold:

\[ \Prob(Z_1\le\tau,\ldots,Z_{i-1}\le\tau) \ge \Prob(\M\le\tau)=1-p(\tau). \]

The discounted structure supplies one extra piece. Since \(Z_{i+1}\overset{d}{=}\gamma Z_i\), we have

\[ c_{i+1}(\tau) = \E[(\gamma Z_i-\tau)^+] \le \gamma\E[(Z_i-\tau)^+] = \gamma c_i(\tau). \]

Here \(\gamma<1\) matters: geometric decay makes the total discounted surplus controllable by the first surplus term. Thus \(C(\tau)\le c_1(\tau)/(1-\gamma)\), or equivalently

\[ c_1(\tau)\ge (1-\gamma)C(\tau). \tag{4}\label{eq:first-surplus-controls-total} \]

Since the reach probability at \(i=1\) is \(1\), combining the reach lower bound with \(\eqref{eq:first-surplus-controls-total}\) gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{i\ge1} \Prob(Z_1\le\tau,\ldots,Z_{i-1}\le\tau)c_i(\tau) &\ge (1-p(\tau))C(\tau)+p(\tau)c_1(\tau) \\ &\ge (1-p(\tau))C(\tau)+p(\tau)(1-\gamma)C(\tau) \\ &= \bigl(1-\gamma p(\tau)\bigr)C(\tau) \\ &\ge \bigl(1-\gamma p(\tau)\bigr)S(\tau). \end{aligned} \]

Substituting this into \(\eqref{eq:discounted-threshold-surplus}\) gives the discounted threshold decomposition

\[ \E[\ALG_\tau] \ge p(\tau)\tau+\bigl(1-\gamma p(\tau)\bigr)S(\tau). \tag{5}\label{eq:discounted-decomposition} \]

The same proof applies to a finite horizon by replacing the sums over \(i\ge1\) with sums over the available times.

Independence is used to factor the reach event from the current observation. Beyond independence, identical distribution enters through the geometric surplus decay \(c_{i+1}(\tau)\le\gamma c_i(\tau)\). Thus the same certificate applies to any independent discounted sequence for which this decay inequality holds for every \(i\) and \(\tau\).

The endpoint \(\gamma=1\) is not obtained by substituting into this proof. Then \(c_1(\tau)\ge(1-\gamma)C(\tau)\) becomes vacuous, and in an undiscounted infinite horizon the sum \(C(\tau)\) need not be finite. This is why the undiscounted endpoint is treated separately below.

Deterministic thresholds

For \(0\le\gamma<1\), the discounted decomposition gives three deterministic fixed-threshold calibrations, all balancing the same inequality in different ways.

The useful certificate is obtained by centering the lower bound at the target value. Since \(\M\le\tau+(\M-\tau)^+\), we have \(S(\tau)\ge\E[\M]-\tau\). Hence, by \(\eqref{eq:discounted-decomposition}\),

\[ \begin{aligned} \E[\ALG_\tau] &\ge p(\tau)\tau+\bigl(1-\gamma p(\tau)\bigr)S(\tau) \\ &\ge p(\tau)\tau+\bigl(1-\gamma p(\tau)\bigr)(\E[\M]-\tau) \\ &= \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]+ \left(p(\tau)-\frac{1}{1+\gamma}\right) \Bigl((1+\gamma)\tau-\gamma\E[\M]\Bigr). \end{aligned} \tag{6}\label{eq:centered-certificate} \]

Discounted mean and tail-probability thresholds

This centered form gives the discounted mean threshold

\[ \tau_{\mathrm{mean}} := \frac{\gamma}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \]

because this value makes the second factor in \(\eqref{eq:centered-certificate}\) vanish. Therefore

\[ \E[\ALG_{\tau_{\mathrm{mean}}}] \ge \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \]

More generally, if \(0\le c\le1\) and \(\tau=c\E[\M]\), then

\[ \E[\ALG_\tau] \ge \min\{1-c,\ 1-\gamma+\gamma c\}\E[\M]. \tag{7}\label{eq:mean-curve} \]

Indeed, substituting \(\tau=c\E[\M]\) into \(\eqref{eq:centered-certificate}\), and then using only \(0\le p(\tau)\le1\), gives the first term \(1-c\) when \((1+\gamma)c-\gamma\ge0\), and the second term \(1-\gamma+\gamma c\) when \((1+\gamma)c-\gamma\le0\).

The two terms in \(\eqref{eq:mean-curve}\) balance when

\[ 1-c=1-\gamma+\gamma c, \qquad c=\frac{\gamma}{1+\gamma}. \]

At this value, both terms are \(1/(1+\gamma)\), matching the factor obtained by the centered certificate.

For \(0<\gamma<1\), another analogue of the median threshold is the tail-probability threshold \(\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}\), chosen so that

\[ p(\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}) = \Prob(\M>\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}) = \frac{1}{1+\gamma}. \]

When this exact level is attainable (otherwise one may use the corresponding quantile with an arbitrarily small independent perturbation and pass to the limit), the first factor in \(\eqref{eq:centered-certificate}\) vanishes at this threshold. Therefore

\[ \E[\ALG_{\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}}] \ge \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \]

More generally, the centered certificate shows that every deterministic fixed threshold between \(\tau_{\mathrm{mean}}\) and \(\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}\) works:

\[ \tau\in \left[ \min\{\tau_{\mathrm{mean}},\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}\}, \max\{\tau_{\mathrm{mean}},\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}\} \right] \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \E[\ALG_\tau]\ge \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \]

Equivalently, \(\eqref{eq:centered-certificate}\) gives

\[ \E[\ALG_\tau] \ge \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M] + \left(p(\tau)-\frac{1}{1+\gamma}\right) \Bigl((1+\gamma)\tau-\gamma\E[\M]\Bigr). \tag{8}\label{eq:interval-certificate} \]

The second factor in \(\eqref{eq:interval-certificate}\) changes sign at

\[ \tau_{\mathrm{mean}}=\frac{\gamma}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \]

The first factor changes sign at \(\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}\), because \(p(\tau_{\mathrm{tail}})=1/(1+\gamma)\) and \(p(\tau)\) is nonincreasing in \(\tau\). Hence the two factors have the same sign throughout the interval between \(\tau_{\mathrm{mean}}\) and \(\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}\), so the product term in \(\eqref{eq:interval-certificate}\) is nonnegative.

Surplus fixed point

A third deterministic calibration is the fixed point

\[ \tau_\star=\gamma S(\tau_\star) = \gamma\E[(\M-\tau_\star)^+]. \]

Existence and uniqueness are immediate from monotonicity. The function \(S(\tau)\) is continuous, nonincreasing, finite, and satisfies \(S(0)=\E[\M]\) and \(S(\tau)\to0\) as \(\tau\to\infty\). Define

\[ h(\tau):=\tau-\gamma S(\tau). \]

Then \(h(0)=-\gamma\E[\M]\le0\), with equality only at the trivial endpoint \(\gamma=0\), while \(h(\tau)\to\infty\). Thus a solution exists. If \(\tau_2>\tau_1\), then

\[ h(\tau_2)-h(\tau_1) = (\tau_2-\tau_1)+\gamma\bigl(S(\tau_1)-S(\tau_2)\bigr) > 0. \]

Hence \(h\) is strictly increasing, so the fixed point is unique.

It also satisfies

\[ \E[\ALG_{\tau_\star}] \ge \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \]

Indeed, substituting \(\tau_\star=\gamma S(\tau_\star)\) into the decomposition gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \E[\ALG_{\tau_\star}] &\ge p(\tau_\star)\tau_\star+ \bigl(1-\gamma p(\tau_\star)\bigr)S(\tau_\star) \\ &= \gamma p(\tau_\star)S(\tau_\star)+ \bigl(1-\gamma p(\tau_\star)\bigr)S(\tau_\star) \\ &= S(\tau_\star). \end{aligned} \]

On the other hand,

\[ \E[\M] \le \tau_\star+S(\tau_\star) = (1+\gamma)S(\tau_\star). \]

Therefore \(S(\tau_\star)\ge\E[\M]/(1+\gamma)\), proving the guarantee.

This also gives another interval. Since \(\tau_\star=\gamma S(\tau_\star)\), the preceding inequality implies \(\tau_\star\ge\tau_{\mathrm{mean}}\). For \(0<\gamma<1\), if \(\tau\in[\tau_{\mathrm{mean}},\tau_\star]\), then \(h(\tau)\le0\), so \(\tau\le\gamma S(\tau)\). Using the decomposition and \(p(\tau)\le1\),

\[ \begin{aligned} \E[\ALG_\tau] &\ge S(\tau)+p(\tau)\bigl(\tau-\gamma S(\tau)\bigr) \\ &\ge \tau+(1-\gamma)S(\tau) \\ &\ge \frac{\tau}{\gamma} \ge \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \end{aligned} \]

Thus every threshold between \(\tau_{\mathrm{mean}}\) and \(\tau_\star\) also works. At \(\gamma=0\), this interval degenerates to \(\tau=0\).

A certified interval of deterministic thresholds

For \(0<\gamma<1\), combining the two deterministic intervals above, the present certificates guarantee the \(1/(1+\gamma)\) bound for every

\[ \tau\in \left[ \min\{\tau_{\mathrm{tail}},\tau_{\mathrm{mean}}\}, \max\{\tau_{\mathrm{tail}},\tau_\star\} \right]. \]

That is, every threshold in this interval satisfies \(\E[\ALG_\tau]\ge\E[\M]/(1+\gamma)\).

A hard instance

The following infinite-horizon instance shows why the factor \(1/(1+\gamma)\) is tight. Fix \(\gamma\in(0,1)\). For a large parameter \(H\), let

\[ X_i= \begin{cases} H, & \text{with probability } p_H,\\ \gamma, & \text{with probability } 1-p_H, \end{cases} \qquad p_H:=\frac{1-\gamma}{H}. \]

Here \(\gamma\) is the decent value that appears almost immediately. The value \(H\) is rare, but there are infinitely many chances to see it before discount erases its contribution.

Online value

Let \(V_H\) be the optimal online value for this instance, allowing arbitrary stopping rules. By stationarity, \(V_H\) satisfies the Bellman equation

\[ V_H=\E[\max\{X_1,\gamma V_H\}]. \]

Stopping immediately gives

\[ \E[X_1] = p_HH+(1-p_H)\gamma = 1-\gamma p_H. \]

For this value, the continuation value is

\[ \gamma(1-\gamma p_H)<\gamma. \]

Thus even the low observation \(\gamma\) is better than continuing, so stopping immediately is optimal. Hence

\[ V_H=1-\gamma p_H\longrightarrow 1 \qquad \text{as }H\to\infty. \tag{9}\label{eq:hard-online} \]

Prophet value

Let

\[ T_H:=\inf\{i\ge1:X_i=H\} \]

be the first high arrival. Then \(T_H\) is geometric with parameter \(p_H\), and the best discounted high value is \(H\gamma^{T_H-1}\). The prophet's payoff is trapped between

\[ \gamma\mathbf{1}\{X_1=\gamma\} + \bigl(H\gamma^{T_H-1}-\gamma\bigr)^+ \le \M \le \gamma+H\gamma^{T_H-1}. \tag{10}\label{eq:hard-prophet-sandwich} \]

The upper bound gives

\[ \E[\M] \le \gamma+ H\E[\gamma^{T_H-1}] = \gamma+ \frac{Hp_H}{1-\gamma(1-p_H)} \longrightarrow \gamma+1. \]

For the lower bound, fix \(K\). For all sufficiently large \(H\), \(H\gamma^{i-1}>\gamma\) for \(i=1,\ldots,K\), and therefore

\[ \begin{aligned} \E\left[\bigl(H\gamma^{T_H-1}-\gamma\bigr)^+\right] &\ge \sum_{i=1}^K p_H(1-p_H)^{i-1}\bigl(H\gamma^{i-1}-\gamma\bigr) \\ &\longrightarrow (1-\gamma)\sum_{i=1}^K\gamma^{i-1}. \end{aligned} \]

Letting \(K\to\infty\), and using \(\E[\gamma\mathbf{1}\{X_1=\gamma\}]\to\gamma\), the lower bound in \(\eqref{eq:hard-prophet-sandwich}\) gives

\[ \liminf_{H\to\infty}\E[\M]\ge \gamma+1. \]

Consequently,

\[ \E[\M]\longrightarrow 1+\gamma. \tag{11}\label{eq:hard-prophet} \]

Combining \(\eqref{eq:hard-online}\) and \(\eqref{eq:hard-prophet}\),

\[ \frac{V_H}{\E[\M]}\longrightarrow \frac{1}{1+\gamma}. \]

The online rule cannot afford to pass up the immediate value \(\gamma\) in search of a very rare \(H\), so its value stays near \(1\). The prophet receives the baseline contribution \(\gamma\), and also captures one unit of expected value from the rare high opportunity spread over infinitely many discounted chances.

Fixed horizon versus infinite horizon

Separate the two roles of the infinite horizon. The guarantee itself does not need infinitely many observations; the infinite horizon is used to show sharpness of the constant.

For a fixed discount \(0<\gamma<1\), the weights \(1,\gamma,\gamma^2,\ldots\) have total mass

\[ 1+\gamma+\gamma^2+\cdots=\frac{1}{1-\gamma}. \]

This is the effective horizon: the number of full-strength opportunities represented by the discounted infinite stream. It is not a hard cutoff; it says that far-future observations still exist, but their combined discounted weight is comparable to finitely many undiscounted observations.

The decomposition proof applies equally to an actual finite horizon \(n\). If

\[ \M_n:=\max_{1\le i\le n}\gamma^{i-1}X_i, \]

and \(\ALG_{n,\tau}\) denotes the realized payoff of the same fixed observed-value threshold \(\tau\), stopped within the first \(n\) observations, then the same argument gives

\[ \sup_{\tau\ge0}\E[\ALG_{n,\tau}] \ge \frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M_n]. \]

Thus for any fixed \(0<\gamma<1\), the same certificate gives the finite-horizon factor \(1/(1+\gamma)\). For fixed finite \(n\), sharper horizon-dependent constants may be possible; already at \(n=1\), the prophet and the online player both face only \(X_1\), so the best ratio is \(1\). The infinite horizon gives the matching obstruction for \(1/(1+\gamma)\). In the finite-horizon statement, the only formal change is which prophet maximum appears: \(\M_n\) replaces \(\M\). The threshold/surplus comparison is otherwise the same.

The hard instance above explains the obstruction. The online rule sees a dependable value \(\gamma\) immediately and must decide whether to stop or wait for a rare value \(H\). The prophet keeps the dependable baseline and also benefits if a rare high value appears early enough.

"Early enough" is the key phrase. A high value arriving at time \(i\) is seen as the observed reward \(H\gamma^{i-1}\). It is useful for the hard instance only while it remains comparable to the low baseline \(\gamma\), namely for times satisfying

\[ H\gamma^{i-1}\gtrsim \gamma. \]

To see the scale of this window, rewrite the inequality as

\[ H\gamma^{i-1}\gtrsim \gamma \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \gamma^{i-1}\gtrsim \frac{\gamma}{H}. \]

Taking logarithms gives

\[ i-1 \lesssim \frac{\log(H/\gamma)}{\log(1/\gamma)}. \]

For fixed \(\gamma\), the additive \(\log(1/\gamma)\) term inside \(\log(H/\gamma)\) is constant, so this is of order

\[ \log_{1/\gamma} H. \]

The logarithm appears because each extra time step multiplies the high value by another factor of \(\gamma\). The high value starts at size \(H\), and it takes about \(\log_{1/\gamma}H\) discount steps before it falls back to the scale of the low value.

A convenient shorthand for the scale of this useful window is

\[ \text{useful window length}\asymp \log_{1/\gamma}H. \]

Read this as scale intuition, not a precise cutoff. For fixed \(n\), the same two-point construction gives a milder obstruction, because the prophet has only the first \(n\) discounted chances to benefit from the rare high value. In the large-\(H\) limit, this construction's prophet value is governed by the partial geometric sum

\[ \gamma+(1-\gamma)(1+\gamma+\cdots+\gamma^{n-1}) = 1+\gamma-\gamma^n, \]

while the online value stays near \(1\). This is only the obstruction from this family, not an exact finite-\(n\) constant; only as \(n\to\infty\) does it approach the infinite-horizon obstruction \(1+\gamma\). Conceptually, the lower bound comes from the discounted surplus decomposition and already works at finite horizon; the matching upper-bound obstruction comes from allowing sufficiently many discounted chances for the rare high value.

The undiscounted endpoint

The endpoint \(\gamma=1\) should be kept conceptually separate because the discounted surplus certificate above uses \(1-\gamma>0\). For \(0<\gamma<1\), the infinite-horizon hard instance forces the ratio \(1/(1+\gamma)\), which tends to \(1/2\) as \(\gamma\uparrow1\). At \(\gamma=1\), the undiscounted infinite-horizon endpoint has different structure and the bounded-support ratio is \(1\).

In the finite undiscounted i.i.d. case, a single fixed threshold already gives a stronger benchmark than \(1/2\). Choose the threshold \(\tau\) at the upper \(1/n\)-quantile of the common distribution, so that each observation crosses with probability \(1/n\). Then the rule accepts some observation with probability \(1-(1-1/n)^n\), and the same threshold/surplus calculation gives

\[ \E[\ALG_{n,\tau}] \ge \left(1-\left(1-\frac1n\right)^n\right)\E[\M_n] \ge \left(1-\frac1e\right)\E[\M_n]. \]

This is the \(1-1/e\) i.i.d. single-threshold guarantee of Hill and Kertz [HK82].

For the standard finite-horizon i.i.d. prophet problem, allowing the full optimal stopping rule improves the universal constant beyond \(1/2\). The sharp constant is \(1/\beta\approx0.745\), where \(\beta>1\) is the unique solution of

\[ \int_0^1 \frac{dy}{y(1-\ln y)+(\beta-1)} = 1. \]

Kertz's i.i.d. prophet-region analysis gives this upper bound and the exact expression for \(\beta\) [Ker86]. Later threshold strategies attain the same constant, so the bound is tight [CFHOV21].

At infinite horizon with \(\gamma=1\) and bounded support, the situation degenerates further. If \(x^\star=\operatorname{ess\,sup}X<\infty\), then the prophet's maximum is \(x^\star\) almost surely. A fixed threshold rule can stop at the first time \(X_i>x^\star-\varepsilon\), which occurs almost surely when that event has positive probability, and obtains at least \(x^\star-\varepsilon\). Letting \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\) gives ratio \(1\). This endpoint sanity check is useful context, but it is not the fixed-\(\gamma<1\) discounted obstruction proved above.

Takeaway

For \(0\le\gamma<1\), the mean and fixed-point calibrations are certified; for \(0<\gamma<1\), the tail-probability calibration is certified as well:

\[ \tau_{\mathrm{mean}} = \frac{\gamma}{1+\gamma}\E[\M], \qquad \Prob(\M>\tau_{\mathrm{tail}}) = \frac{1}{1+\gamma}, \qquad \tau_\star = \gamma\E[(\M-\tau_\star)^+]. \]

Each certified threshold guarantees

\[ \E[\ALG_\tau]\ge\frac{1}{1+\gamma}\E[\M]. \]

For \(0<\gamma<1\), the two-point hard instance above shows that this constant is tight: even the optimal online stopping rule has value asymptotic to \(\E[\M]/(1+\gamma)\).

The discounted mean threshold optimizes a worst-case lower envelope in \(\eqref{eq:mean-curve}\). For \(0<\gamma<1\), the tail-probability threshold makes the threshold and surplus coefficients in \(\eqref{eq:discounted-decomposition}\) equal. The surplus fixed point makes the \(p(\tau)\)-dependent part cancel. Thus the guarantee is not tied to a single threshold; it comes from the common discounted decomposition

\[ \E[\ALG_\tau] \ge p(\tau)\tau+\bigl(1-\gamma p(\tau)\bigr)S(\tau). \]

This is the discounted analogue of the same threshold/surplus principle.
